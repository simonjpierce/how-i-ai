# The science workflow

Run a research project end to end — the analysis, and the paper that comes out of it — with the AI doing the drafting while you steer and check. It has built-in answers to the two questions everyone asks the first time they trust an AI with serious work: *how do I know it's right?* and *how do I know it isn't making things up?*

This is an idea file. Paste it into Claude Code to build a version for your own work; it'll fill in the specifics (folder names, exact tools) in collaboration with you.

*What you'll need: the rigour core leans on a second AI model on the command line — a separate install and account, and central here rather than optional-feeling. Without one you still get the lab-notebook discipline and the citation checks, but not the independent cross-check that answers "how do I know it's right" — and a faithful build says so plainly rather than faking a review. Worth setting the second model up before you lean on this for anything headed for publication.*

## The core idea

The usual way to do analysis with an AI is one long chat: you paste data, talk through tests, copy results into a document at the end. The failure mode is familiar — forty messages in, the model quietly contradicts an earlier decision, you can't remember which approach you settled on, and the numbers in the write-up have drifted from what the code actually produced. The conversation was the record, and conversations are lossy.

The fix is to stop treating the chat as the record. Instead the AI keeps a **lab notebook** as it works — one markdown file that is the authoritative, step-by-step account of the analysis: what was done, what was found, what it means, where each output lives. It's written *during* the work, one step at a time, not reconstructed afterwards. The **manuscript** is then a derivative of the notebook. The discipline that holds it together: **every step gets logged before the next one starts**, and the AI does the writing — you read along, steer, and verify near the end rather than typing into the files yourself.

It works for any kind of paper — a primary analysis, a review, an evidence synthesis. There's always a method of some description, and always decisions about what to include; that's the part this makes disciplined.

## How it runs

Build it as a skill with **two modes** — *lab-notebook* mode for live research sessions, and *manuscript* mode for drafting the paper from a finished notebook.

**Lab-notebook mode.** The AI sets up a project folder (the notebook, a manuscript scaffold, and — if there's code — a version-controlled repo for it) and then keeps the notebook as you work. After every meaningful step it records, before moving on: what was done (method and parameters), the key numbers, what it means, where the outputs live, and the outcome of the two checks below. By default **every step that produces a result or changes a claim goes through those checks**; you can mark a stretch of work "exploratory" to skip them, but the skip is logged in the notebook with the reason — rigour is never dropped silently.

**Settle the foundation before you model.** One sequencing rule sits above the per-step checks: before fitting any structural model or running a simulation, the AI pins down what's actually being estimated and whether the model's assumptions hold — against both the data and the biology — and gets you to confirm it. A simulation can validate a model rigorously and still be validating the *wrong* model; settling the question, the estimand, and the assumptions first is cheap insurance against a precise answer to the wrong question.

**The two checks — the heart of it.** Each significant step runs a small loop: *propose the method → review it independently before running → run it → verify the numbers after → write the notebook entry.*

- **An independent second-model cross-check.** The skill dispatches to a *second AI model in its own command-line tool* — the Codex CLI or the Gemini CLI work well — separate from the model doing the analysis, and **read-only** so it can't change anything. *Before* the step, it gets what it needs to judge — the relevant prior notebook, the data (or source) structure, the planned method, its assumptions, the expected output — and proposes its own method, returning a clear verdict: agree, agree-with-a-caveat, or disagree. A caveat or disagreement pauses for your call rather than being smoothed over. *After* the step, it checks each number against the actual output, quoting the source line; anything it can't confirm is a discrepancy that pauses the work until it's resolved or explicitly noted. If you have no second model available, the skill says so in the notebook — it never pretends a review happened. *This is the answer to "how do I know it's right": a different model checks the work, not the one that produced it.*

- **Citation verification.** Every reference is checked against the scholarly databases — Semantic Scholar, CrossRef, and OpenAlex — and comes back found, not-found, or ambiguous, with the evidence. Not-found means *possibly fabricated*: it's flagged for your attention, never auto-deleted. AI models do invent realistic-looking references; this catches them before a co-author or reviewer does. Worth building as its own reusable citation-check step that this workflow calls.

For non-code work — a review or synthesis — "the actual output" the checks read against isn't a script log but your auditable record: extraction tables, screening logs, database exports, the source PDFs themselves (with page or line references). Same discipline, different evidence. And how much rigour to apply scales to the work: a quick exploratory pass needs less than a result headed for publication.

**One more check, on demand — and what makes it possible.** The cross-checks above run continuously and lightly. Alongside them, at any point where a result really matters — and again before the paper goes out — you can pause and point a deliberately *hostile* review at the whole analysis: a fresh pass by the independent second model at its highest reasoning effort, plus a separate agent told to try to *break* the result rather than confirm it. It's the per-step cross-check turned up and aimed at the analysis as a whole, and running it is your call, not automatic — a heavy adversarial pass on every step just trains you to tune it out, while an occasional deliberate one stays sharp. For that review to mean anything — and for a real co-author or reviewer to be able to check your work — the analysis has to be reproducible from the repository alone. So the standing habit is to push *everything* needed to rerun each result — the code, the input data, and the saved outputs — to the project's repository, not just the code; the test is that someone handed only the repository link could rerun it and land on the same numbers. (Where the data is sensitive — the precise locations of a threatened species, say — keep the repository private and coarsen or hold back the risky detail, noting what was held back.)

**Manuscript mode.** The AI drafts the paper from the notebook. The methods and results come first, because they follow fairly directly from the analysis decisions — it can largely write them itself. For the introduction and discussion it interviews you, asking what it needs to draft that text *with* you rather than expecting you to write it cold. To set the shape, it asks up front whether there's a paper whose structure you're aiming for, and uses that as the manuscript scaffold. And as your own habits accumulate in the vault — the shape your introductions tend to take, the way you report results, the small house rules down to whether you use Oxford commas — it drafts to *your* structure rather than a generic one. That's the quiet payoff of keeping the record this way: the version you submit is the one you'd have written, reached far faster, because you're editing from your own externalised mental model instead of starting from a blank page. Before anything is shared it runs the checks that catch the usual errors: every result traces to a method and every method to a result; every key number matches across notebook, manuscript, and outputs; key estimates carry uncertainty or a stated limitation; language stays no stronger than the evidence; and citations are verified before it goes to a co-author or reviewer.

**Make it read like you — without breaking the science.** A verified, traceable draft can still read like a machine wrote it: correct, evenly grey, none of the rhythm a real author has. The final pass fixes that, under one hard rule — the prose may change, the science may not. It starts with a *voice guide*: a short reference the AI builds from your own published writing, describing how you actually write — not "be more casual" but the measurable things, like how much your sentence lengths vary, how and how often you hedge, the words you never use. (The maintainer's was distilled from his own papers and book chapters, down to a target spread of sentence lengths.) Built once, it becomes the calibration target every later draft is held to, and it works for anything you write, not just papers.

Then the same draft → independent-check → you-decide loop runs, but aimed at *prose* now. The AI rewrites a section to your voice; a second model, read-only, checks the rewrite against the original — not "is this good writing?" (it can't judge your voice and shouldn't try) but "did the rewrite quietly change anything it shouldn't?" — a number that moved, a citation that drifted, a hedge that hardened from *suggests* to *shows*. Beneath it a blunt mechanical check pulls every number out of the before and the after and diffs the two lists, so anything that slipped is caught before you ever see the draft. Only then does the AI bring you the few genuine judgement calls — a word, an emphasis, a sentence that could go two ways — for your ear to settle.

The belt and braces are there for a failure mode that's easy to miss: rewriting prose for flow reliably nudges the content too — a "may" becomes a "does", a caveat falls off the end of a sentence — so the writing improves while the meaning silently drifts. An independent fidelity check, with a number-for-number diff under it, is what lets the prose be reworked hard without the results moving an inch.

## What this does *not* do

It doesn't choose your statistics or design your analysis — picking the model, the test, the framing is yours, and it's field-specific. What it gives you is a session structure and a record-keeping discipline that work the same whether you do mark–recapture, phylogenetics, a meta-analysis, or field tallies. And it doesn't replace your judgement on what it surfaces: the calls on a flagged caveat, a model disagreement, or a possibly-fabricated citation are yours to make. It won't quietly fix a discrepancy or pretend an independent review happened when it didn't. Keep your own methods; let the workflow keep the record honest.

## Why this works

Self-review is weak — a model that just made an error is the worst-placed to catch it. An independent second model, run read-only against the actual output, catches what self-review can't. And the bookkeeping that rigour really depends on — logging every decision, keeping results tied to outputs, never letting an unverified number into the write-up — is exactly the tedious work people skip under time pressure and pay for later. The AI doesn't get bored doing it, and the step-gate makes skipping it the harder path. The notebook stays trustworthy because keeping it trustworthy is nearly free, and the paper is only ever as strong as what's traceable underneath it.

## Note

This is a pattern, not a fixed implementation. The parts that are yours to shape: where projects live and how you back up any analysis code; whether the work involves code at all (a review may not); which second model and CLI you use for the independent check; and how heavily to lean on the rigour. It assumes Claude Code as the main driver. The durable idea is: *the notebook is the record, a second model checks the first, and the paper is only ever as strong as what's traceable underneath it.* Paste this to your AI and build the version that fits how you work.
